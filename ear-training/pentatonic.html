Ear Training

Develop your musical ear for banjo and bluegrass

Instructions

This ear training exercise helps you develop the ability to identify notes within the G major scale patterns commonly used in banjo playing. Choose between the traditional pentatonic scale (fundamental to bluegrass) or the full G major scale for more comprehensive training.

This exercise is different from any other I know of because it's banjo specific. Traditional ear training seems designed for singers or keyboard players but doesn't handle the way a banjo actually works. The physicality of playing is crucial to how I learn, so this test is designed to connect the sounds with the physical locations on the banjo fretboard. There are multiple ways to play the same note on the banjo, so the test includes all the common positions for each note in the scale, which helps you develop a more flexible and comprehensive understanding of the instrument.

Note: The pentatonic mode includes C4 even though it's not part of the G major pentatonic scale (G, A, B, D, E). This note commonly appears as a passing tone in bluegrass banjo playing and sits naturally on the 1st fret of the B string in the standard pentatonic box pattern.

Note: You can add F# as well to give you a full G major scale. This note is the leading tone in the key of G and creates a strong sense of resolution back to the root note G, making melodies more directional and harmonically rich.

Note: This is not an absolute pitch test, unless you want it to be - the notes will be transposed based on your capo position, so you can practice identifying intervals and scale degrees within the context of your current key. If you want to do absolute pitch testing then deselect the reference note and try to identify the notes without any reference point.

Don't have a banjo with you just now? At the bottom of the page you can also use the fretboard buttons to play notes with your mouse or touch, and you can mix and match instruments for the tests and fretboard to challenge your ear with different timbres.

How can this help

This exercise is designed to be used with your banjo in hand, so you can connect the sounds you're hearing with the physical locations on the fretboard. By practicing regularly, you'll start to internalize the sound of each note and its relationship to the others, which will help you learn songs faster and play by ear more effectively.

Although you select the note by the letter name the selector mimics the banjo fret board layout, so for example the A note is located on the 2nd fret of the G string, and the E note is on the 2nd fret of the D string. By associating the sound of each note with its position on the fretboard, you'll develop a stronger mental map of the instrument, which is crucial for improvisation and playing by ear.

When you play the test note, try to recall how that note sounds in relation to the other notes in the scale. For example, if you hear the A note, think about how it sounds in relation to the G (root) and B (major third) notes. Over time, you'll develop a stronger sense of relative pitch, which is crucial for playing by ear and improvising on the banjo.

Practice Tips:

I play the scale a few times first, from the lowest to the highest test note, to give myself a reference for the sound of each note. Then I focus on the relationships between the notes - for example, how the A note sounds in relation to the G, or how the E note relates to the D. Over time, you'll start to recognize these relationships more quickly, which will help you identify intervals by ear when you're playing or listening to music.

I always try and reproduce the sound by selecting the corresponding note on the banjo. The Character of Each Note

Each note in the scale has its own sound and emotional feel, independent of what it's called. Learning to hear these qualities — rather than just memorising names — is the key to playing by ear.

The home note
Settled, arrived, at rest. This is the note everything keeps returning to. When a phrase lands here it feels complete — there's nowhere left to go.
The step away
One easy step up from home. Bright and light, it moves forward without creating tension. It's the note that starts a gentle climb but isn't committed to it yet.
The sunshine note
The note that makes this scale sound major. Warm and open — when you hear it over a G chord it locks in that clear, happy quality. This is the note that defines the character of the key more than any other.
The passing tone
Slightly unsettled, this note doesn't like to sit still. In bluegrass it works as a bridge — moving through on the way to somewhere more stable rather than landing and staying. It adds momentum and interest without feeling resolved.
The anchor
Strong and stable, but not quite home. The second most restful note in the key — it can sit comfortably in a melody without demanding to move, yet it still carries a subtle pull back toward the root. Often works as a midpoint of rest in a phrase.
The colour note
Open and airy with a slightly sweet quality. It lifts a phrase and adds brightness without tension. Less grounded than the anchor but less tense than the pull — it often appears near the top of a run, giving the melody an expansive feeling.
The pull
The most tense note in the scale. It leans hard toward the home note and almost demands to resolve upward. Playing this note and then landing on the root creates one of the strongest feelings of arrival in the key. Use it deliberately — it gives melodies direction and finality.
Home again
The same feeling as the root, but brighter and higher — like arriving back at the same place after a full journey. Melodies that climb all the way here feel expansive and complete. It reinforces everything the home note stands for, just with more energy.
The deep anchor
The same note as the anchor above — D, the 5th of the key — but heard from below home, a perfect fourth away. That stable, grounded character carries across registers: it still feels restful and settled, just weightier and deeper, with a gentle pull upward toward home.
The low colour
The same note as the colour note above — E, the 6th of the key — but a minor third below home. The sweetness is still recognisable, but sitting this close below the root gives it more warmth and intimacy. It's an inviting place to approach home from, with a natural pull upward.
The doorstep
One half-step below home — so close you can almost feel the arrival before it happens. Like the pull above, it leans hard toward the root, but from beneath. Starting or landing here creates immediate upward tension that almost demands to resolve to the home note.
